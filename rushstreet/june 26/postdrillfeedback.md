# Rushstreet Post-Drill Feedback Themes

Source: `rushstreet_all_drills_usage.csv`

Scope: drills experienced since Jan 1 2026. This summary uses the player-volunteered `surprises` and `learnedToday` columns separately. Blank and `n/a` responses are excluded from the thematic summary.

## Surprises

The `surprises` responses mostly describe what felt newly salient, unexpected, or difficult in the drill scenario itself.

### 1. Problems were often less visible or less straightforward than expected

Several players were surprised by how much investigation was needed before the shape of the incident became clear. The feedback points to weak initial visibility, hidden root causes, or unclear severity.

- milisavljevic, `The Chain Workshop`: "I don't see visible problem"
- milisavljevic, `The Switch`: "That issue is so long ongoing, no one noticed till now"
- armin400, `Details Matter`: "Issue wasnt straightforward, further investigation was required"
- nezhadian, `Bezs Beatles Day!`: "the visibility is not enough"

### 2. Multiple changes and dependencies made rollback decisions harder

Players noticed that incidents were not always caused by a single obvious change. In several drills, multiple changes, third-party factors, or uncertainty around rollback side effects complicated the response.

- armin400, `The Chain Workshop`: "Multiple changes, 1 rollback didnt help"
- armin400, `Cart-astrophe`: "Multiple changes caused issues"
- milisavljevic, `Details Matter`: "Third party including"

### 3. Incident severity and urgency could shift during the scenario

The drills created situations where impact evolved over time, or where the participant had to judge whether the issue was critical enough to escalate.

- armin400, `The Switch`: "intermittent issues and then increased to higher severity issue"
- milisavljevic, `The Switch`: "it was not clear is it critical or not"
- armin400, `Version confusion`: "Everything was going as planned"

### 4. Coordination friction was a repeated surprise

Players were caught by people being unavailable, not responding, or not wanting to declare/escalate an incident. The Snipe Hunt responses especially show that coordination failure became part of the challenge.

- milisavljevic, `Version confusion`: "That people didn't want incident"
- milisavljevic, `Snipe Hunt`: "That everyone is OOO, it was hard to get people"
- armin400, `Snipe Hunt`: "Multiple necessary people were Out"
- armin400, `Snipe Hunt`: "had to word directly \"I approve the restarts\""

### 5. Some later drills were less surprising, suggesting growing familiarity

Not every response describes surprise. A few notes suggest that some players were becoming more comfortable with the drill pattern or with the incident-response expectations.

- armin400, `Version confusion`: "nothing really surprising this time"
- milisavljevic, `Cart-astrophe`: "Not surprised at all"

## Learned Today

The `learnedToday` responses are more action-oriented. Players use this field to name concrete habits they would carry into future incidents.

### 1. Investigate more deeply before acting

The most consistent learning is that the first visible symptom may not be the real problem. Players repeatedly mention gathering information, looking for root cause, and avoiding premature conclusions.

- milisavljevic, `The Chain Workshop`: "Issue could lay anywhere and deeper investigation is needed"
- armin400, `The Switch`: "gather as much info as possible"
- armin400, `Details Matter`: "DO not rush and gather all info possible"
- milisavljevic, `Details Matter`: "Sometimes you need root cause over resolution"

### 2. Own the incident and keep moving toward resolution

Players also volunteered lessons about taking command, taking responsibility, and staying steady under pressure.

- armin400, `The Chain Workshop`: "To take command and fix the issue ASAP"
- milisavljevic, `Snipe Hunt`: "taking responsibility is sometimes best course of action"
- armin400, `Snipe Hunt`: "Stay calm"
- armin400, `Snipe Hunt`: "keep moving steadily towards resolution"

### 3. Coordinate explicitly and include the right people

Several learnings focus on team involvement: clarifying roles, bringing in relevant engineers, asking people to check twice, and including advisors before risky rollback actions.

- armin400, `The Switch`: "Make sure who is responsible of what role"
- armin400, `Version confusion`: "Included all the teams that might have any insights"
- milisavljevic, `Version confusion`: "ask people to check twice and focus"
- milisavljevic, `Cart-astrophe`: "include all engineers if relevant one is not available"
- armin400, `Cart-astrophe`: "include Maya before we rollback"

### 4. Check risk before high-impact actions

Rollback and restart decisions stand out as places where players learned to slow down, inspect risk, and consider wider side effects.

- milisavljevic, `The Switch`: "To not restart without checking risk"
- milisavljevic, `The Switch`: "observe wider picture of issue and timeframe"
- armin400, `Cart-astrophe`: Maya "should be able to advise regarding side effects of rollback"

### 5. Escalation depends on clearer impact

One response directly connects priority escalation to impact clarity. This fits the broader theme that players are learning to build a stronger evidence base before changing the incident posture.

- armin400, `Version confusion`: "incidents may escalate to higher priorities if the impect is more clear"

## Overall Readout

Rushstreet players' volunteered feedback shows a shift from solving the visible symptom toward managing the incident system around it: deeper diagnosis, clearer ownership, better coordination, risk-aware actions, and calmer progression toward resolution. The `surprises` column shows what made drills feel hard or realistic; the `learnedToday` column shows the practical response habits players took from those experiences.
