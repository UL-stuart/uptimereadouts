## Narrative Summary

Andrew's dominant strength was procedural reliability: he raised the incident record promptly, communicated scope to the business early and honestly, delegated diagnostic work to the right people rather than attempting hands-on investigation, and closed the incident with a verified, grounded statement. His tone remained calm and his coordination instincts were sound — he consistently stayed above the line and routed information between responders when it was needed.

His most significant gap was passive sensemaking: Andrew never externalized a working hypothesis to the team, never articulated the evolving picture of the incident, and relied almost entirely on other team members to generate, test, and revise frames. Each shift in his focus — from "everything is normal" to load tests to front-end changes to security — was triggered by a prompt from someone else rather than from his own pattern recognition or anomaly detection. This meant he was following the team's sensemaking rather than leading it.

Andrew's performance was broadly consistent from start to finish — competent and calm, but without a clear upward arc. The closing phase contained a misstep (proposing a customer apology message without compliance input) that suggested he had not fully internalized the novel compliance dimension introduced by the confirmed DoS attack, and which required public correction from maya.

The single most impactful change for next time would be to narrate his mental model out loud at regular intervals — statements like "my current read is that this looks like X because of Y — does anyone see it differently?" This practice would transform Andrew from a competent coordinator of others' sensemaking into a genuine driver of shared situational awareness, expose working hypotheses to challenge before they produce premature actions, and signal to the team that intellectual leadership is expected from the IC chair.
