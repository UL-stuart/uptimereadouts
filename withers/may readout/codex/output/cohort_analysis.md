# Cohort Incident Response Analysis

## Executive Summary

Across the cohort, incident lifecycle basics were the strongest shared capability. Most players raised or inherited a formal incident record, identified customer impact, moved toward restoration, and checked recovery before communicating service return. The weaker shared pattern was command-level cognition: players often asked useful questions and delegated well, but they did not consistently narrate the current hypothesis, confidence, owner, decision point, and fallback in a way that made the whole response system smarter.

The cohort average scores were strongest in Incident Lifecycle Management (4.06) and Coordination (3.88), with lower performance in Decision-Making Under Uncertainty (3.35), Sensemaking (3.59), and Communication (3.65). This suggests the group can run the mechanics of an incident, but should now focus on the higher-leverage IC behaviors that turn activity into shared understanding and calibrated action.

## Cohort Focus Area 1: Make Shared Sensemaking Explicit

The cohort's most common gap is not lack of investigation; it is lack of visible synthesis. Players asked many useful questions, but often left the team to infer the current model from a stream of requests. This matters because responders cannot efficiently challenge weak frames, spot contradictions, or coordinate parallel work if the IC does not narrate what the incident currently appears to be.

Evidence across the cohort:

- Andrew repeatedly gathered useful data but did not consistently synthesize it. In Discount Disaster he moved from normal checks to possible security involvement, asking "have we made any changes this morning" and later "maya can you check you end and see if we have any security issues please." Those were useful moves, but the team did not receive a clear IC summary such as "discount traffic and load tests are not explaining this; security is now the leading hypothesis."
- Lewrad's The Big Investor analysis shows the cost of a weak frame. Lewrad said "daniel shay this looks likely an issue with cart service," while later evidence pointed toward shipping-service scripts and chaos tests. The issue was eventually resolved, but the frame revision was mostly supplied by others.
- Patric's The Unknown Unknown session showed better investigative breadth, moving from "ordering system" mismatch to image/description mismatch and then cache/database corruption. The transcript included good diagnostic moves such as "can we please check the cache for errors in product descriptions?", but the analysis still found that concise shared reframing was limited.
- Nestor's DEFCON session showed a delayed shared frame. Others surfaced the ransomware evidence first: Maya said "Confirmed. This is a ransomware breach," and only later did Nestor tell business "This email is relevant as it looks like we have ransomware on our systems."

Recommended practice:

- Introduce a short IC state-of-play message every major pivot: current hypothesis, evidence, contradictions, owner, next check, and fallback.
- Make disconfirming evidence explicit. If rollback fails, say what has been ruled out and where attention moves next.
- Treat "thinking out loud" as a coordination tool, not a commentary luxury.

## Cohort Focus Area 2: Add IC Structure Through Roles, Cadence, Timeboxes, and Decision Points

Coordination scores were generally strong because players recruited named responders and stayed mostly above the line. The next improvement is to make that coordination more structured. Many incidents had useful delegation but weak ownership boundaries, few timeboxes, and limited responder check-in cadence.

Evidence across the cohort:

- Andrew's early Too Good To Be True session used loose recruitment: "Can we get tanya to have a look" and "can you have a look daniel." Later sessions improved with named asks, but still rarely included timeboxes or explicit ownership.
- Jamie showed the strongest cadence behavior. In The Big Investor, Jamie told business, "This is global and we are investigating, I will report back in 5 mins." In Discount Disaster, Jamie said the site was globally impacted and "I will report back in 10 minutes." This helped stakeholders understand when the next update would arrive.
- Lewrad also used update cadence well in business communication, including "Status: Investigation is ongoing, next update 5 minutes" and "will update again in 5 minutes." The remaining gap was responder-side cadence: technical workstreams were delegated, but not always given explicit deadlines or decision thresholds.
- Patric coordinated broadly, especially in DEFCON and Discount Disaster, but often routed information through themselves rather than structuring the team. In Discount Disaster, Patric moved from "maya any security issues?" to "Daniel Shay Newbie Hamed Silatani can you provide the IP?" and "maya can you stop that ip?" These were effective asks, but the response would be stronger with owners, timeboxes, and a fallback if IP blocking did not restore service.

Recommended practice:

- Assign named owners to parallel tracks: impact/comms, app/service investigation, platform/infrastructure, security/compliance, and restoration validation.
- Timebox investigations: "Report back in five minutes; if no causal evidence, we move to rollback or containment."
- Establish decision points before work begins: what outcome will trigger rollback, maintenance mode, traffic blocking, private security response, or customer communication.

## Cohort Focus Area 3: Improve Trade-Off Management and Calibrated Decisions Under Uncertainty

Decision-making under uncertainty was the cohort's lowest average scoring area. Players were usually willing to act, which is good, but the action was not always paired with explicit risk framing. The cohort needs to preserve bias toward action while making the bet clearer: what is known, what is uncertain, what risk the action carries, who must approve it, and what the fallback is.

Evidence across the cohort:

- Jamie gave one of the clearest positive examples in The Unknown Unknown: "we may need to halt new orders temporarily to fix this issue... otherwise we risk spending more to rectify the issue." This named a trade-off between short-term sales interruption and wider customer/financial harm.
- Patric showed both strong and weak examples. In The Unknown Unknown, Patric asked for "the safest and quickest solution" before choosing database rehydration, which is a good decision frame. In DEFCON, Patric authorized "Can we kill the script please" before the ransomware consequences were understood, and the transcript immediately showed the ransom doubling after the kill attempt. That is the clearest evidence that fast action without calibrated risk can worsen the incident.
- Andrew's The Unknown Unknown session showed a smaller version of the same pattern. He paused orders with business authorization, which was good, but later chose to "try the restart first" before accepting the stronger database rehydration path. The issue was resolved, but the decision did not clearly state criteria, risk, or fallback.
- Lewrad's Discount Disaster response approved blocking the hostile IP effectively, but the analysis noted that communication about the security nature of the incident was controlled without a clear risk/compliance rationale. The action worked, but the decision frame was thin.
- Nestor's DEFCON baseline showed the opposite failure mode: too much reliance on others to frame the decision. Maya raised the core trade-off, saying killing the script could cause "higher ransom or losing data forever," while Nestor did not visibly own the decision structure.

Recommended practice:

- Before irreversible actions, ask: what could get worse, what evidence do we need, and who must approve?
- State confidence explicitly: "I think this is X, confidence medium, because Y; we will verify by Z."
- Name trade-offs in business language: restoration speed vs. data loss, customer transparency vs. security sensitivity, rollback speed vs. evidence preservation, containment vs. regional/customer impact.

## Closing Recommendations

The cohort is ready to move beyond checklist execution into sharper incident command practice. The next training cycle should emphasize three drills: one for shared sensemaking summaries, one for role/cadence/timebox discipline, and one for decision trade-offs under uncertainty. The highest-leverage habit is a repeating IC update format: "Current frame, evidence, owner, next decision, risk, fallback." If the cohort adopts that single behavior consistently, it will improve sensemaking, coordination, communication, and decision quality at the same time.
